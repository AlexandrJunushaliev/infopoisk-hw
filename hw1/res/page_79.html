Personal History
March 19, 2012 Issue

Scars

A life in injuries.

By David Owen

March 12, 2012

On a hill in the neighborhood where I grew up, in Kansas City, was a suburban ruin that my friends and I called the Burned-Down House. There was a crumbling tennis court, which was enclosed by an overgrown chain-link fence, and there was a concrete slab with a dirt-floored crawl space underneath it, and there were two limestone chimneys. During the summer of 1972, when I was in high school, my friend Duncan and I sat on top of one of the chimneys lighting firecrackers with our cigarettes and throwing them at two other friends, who were sitting on a limestone retaining wall and throwing firecrackers at us. Between explosions, we tried to think of something less boring to do. Most of our firecrackers were Black Cats, but we had some cherry bombs, too, and one of those blew up a few inches above my left foot. When I could hear again, and when Duncan and I had stopped laughing, I noticed a nickel-size piece of cherry-bomb shrapnel embedded in the rubber toe cap of my sneaker. When I pulled on it, it came out of my foot like a cork, and blood spread up through the canvas and into the laces. Duncan drove me to the office of my doctor, a pediatrician. The waiting room was full of mothers and weepy three-year-olds, and I took off my shoe and handed it to the receptionist, to show her what the problem was.

I can still make out the line of the wound. Over the years, your body becomes a kind of historical document, in which certain dramatic moments are memorialized in scar tissue. There’s a blemish on my left arm that was caused by a dollop of molten G.I. Joe—the artifact of experiments that my friends and I conducted, in grade school, on the melting points of our possessions. On my right arm, I have two similar marks, made by metal pins that a surgeon inserted on either side of a broken wrist, when I was in college. (After the pins were out and the cast was off, I showed the surgeon that I couldn’t bend my right wrist as much as my left. He tapped the right wrist and said, “This is normal. The other one bends too much.”) When I smile, I have a dimple in my right cheek. Age and extra pounds have made it less prominent, but it’s still visible, and I can feel the other side of it with my tongue. I got it when I was three, by falling from the seat of my tricycle, on which I’d been standing. My main memento of that accident, other than the dimple, is a photograph from the next day’s Kansas City Star of a forlorn-looking little girl, who’s wearing a party dress and pressing one ear of a stuffed lamb to her cheek. The photograph was part of an Easter feature, and I was supposed to have been in it, too. (The writer was a friend of our mothers’.) The caption says the girl is “lonesome for a special playmate,” who hurt himself that morning, and for several years that page in my scrapbook was my favorite. The picture spoke of my ability to influence the emotions of others, as well as suggesting a kind of celebrity. The girl and I didn’t get married—an early assumption of mine, partly clipping-based—although I did take her as my date to my first school dance, in seventh grade. I was ashamed of how skinny I was in those days, and I wore two pairs of pants, one over the other, in the hope of making my legs appear less avian. The last song was the Beatles’ “Hey Jude,” which I later described to another friend’s mother as “a seven-minute slow dance,” hoping to shock her. Many years later, my date’s daughter and my own daughter were college classmates, sorority sisters, and friends. Now they’re both married.

When my daughter was ten, a careless boy at the ice rink cut the back of her hand with his skate, and I drove her to the emergency room to be sewn up. She was frightened and in tears, so on our way to the hospital I told her that some of my happiest, most vivid memories involved accidents. I showed her my dimple and told her about the scars on my arms and my big toe, and I ran down most of the rest of my inventory of falls and fractures and stitches and chipped teeth. I suggested that we think of her hurt hand not as a tragedy but as a potentially interesting adventure, which we would both remember with more than ordinary clarity, and by the time we got to the hospital she had cheered up. Just outside the entrance to the emergency room, we saw a middle-aged man in a white hospital coat smoking a cigarette; he turned out to be the doctor. I could see, as he worked on my daughter’s hand, that his nails and the ends of his fingers were stained by tar, but he was gentle. He used a syringe to stream Novocain into the open wound and let a puddle of it sit for a few minutes, to numb her hand before he stuck her to inject the rest. Meanwhile, he chatted with us about one thing and another, and the reek of tobacco reminded me, pleasantly, of my father. My daughter still has the scar, and she remembers that night with contentment.

Duncan and I were classmates from first grade through high school. He had red hair, freckles, and a round, happy face, and years later—when he was twenty and thirty and forty and fifty—he looked more like his smiling first-grade self than almost anyone else in our class picture. At first, for reasons I don’t remember, we called each other Guee-Guee. (The spelling is speculative; at the time, we couldn’t spell.) We occasionally sat together on the school bus, and when we were in first grade we made up a song—which we tried to sing like Louis Armstrong, whom we’d watched on Ed Sullivan—about the women we saw waiting at city bus stops, on their way to do the cleaning in houses like ours.

The car that Duncan drove me and my injured toe to the doctor in was a white 1963 Chevrolet station wagon, which his family called the Economy Wagon. Duncan had two older brothers and an older sister, and the Economy Wagon had received hard use for a long time. Its odometer, by the time Duncan got his license, had frozen at more than a hundred thousand miles. Its steering wheel pulled hard to the left, so that to go straight you had to turn it hard to the right. The passenger side of the front seat had become unmoored; it slid forward in its track when you slowed down, and slid backward when you accelerated again—a half-scissoring motion that a driver had to anticipate and compensate for, by adjusting pedal pressure. Duncan’s parents had decided to let the Economy Wagon die, and had even stopped changing the oil, but it continued to run, somehow, through all the years we were in high school and, I think, for a few years after that.

A month or two before my toe accident, a few of us rolled down the Economy Wagon’s back-seat windows and filled the openings with rectangles of paperboard, in the center of each of which we’d punched a hole just big enough for a bottle rocket. A classmate of ours had a newer Chevrolet, a sedan, and we modified it the same way, then drove around Kansas City for a couple of hours shooting bottle rockets at each other and trying to throw firecrackers and smoke bombs into the other car’s open front-seat windows. The best way to throw a projectile from a car is, counterintuitively, from the side farther away from what you want to hit, over the roof, with an underhand motion, which we called a Hawaiian flick. To throw an egg at someone, you pull up next to him at a stoplight and Hawaiian-flick the egg over your own roof. The victim angrily looks all around, but when he glares at your car he sees only your passenger, calmly smoking and gazing straight ahead, behind a rolled-up window. At some point, the guys in the other car managed to throw a cherry bomb into the Economy Wagon, and the explosion was so loud, inside that enclosed space, that for a few seconds my mind went utterly blank. Today, my ears ring, perhaps partly as a result—an aural scar—although the ringing didn’t begin until I was fifty. My mother’s mother had ear trouble, too, but much worse than mine. She sometimes blamed her growing deafness on an early admirer, who took her duck-hunting in a rowboat shortly before the beginning of the First World War and steadied his shotgun on her shoulder.

The older brother of one of Duncan’s and my friends had a hydrant wrench—the tool that firefighters use to turn on fire hydrants—which he had found somewhere, or, more likely, had lifted from a fire truck, perhaps one that was responding to a false alarm at his own house. At some point, he bequeathed it to us. We used it to remove the heavy iron cap from a hydrant down the street from Duncan’s house, and we bored a quarter-inch hole through the cap on the drill press in someone’s father’s basement workshop. Then we put the cap back on the hydrant and turned on the water, which came out of the hole like the laser beam in “Goldfinger.” We drove the Economy Wagon back and forth through the beam, and when the water hit the doors it made a noise like a machine gun. We let a few strangers drive through it, too.

A street that connected to the street that ran in front of Duncan’s house dropped off steeply beyond the driveway of the Burned-Down House, and very late on a winter weekend night when the temperature was below freezing we turned on the hydrant at the top of the hill and let it run for a while. In the morning, the entire street was covered with ice, and we went sledding. You could achieve such tremendous speeds that you had to be careful, toward the bottom of the hill, to jerk your sled sharply to the right, into a driveway and across a yard, so that you didn’t slide into four lanes of traffic. On some runs, we went down two sleds at a time, side by side, and had chicken fights. My friend Henry and I got each other into headlocks, then veered off together and hit the curb about halfway down the hill. I took part of the impact with my face, and I could feel one of my front teeth scrape against a metal strut on my sled, which was my father’s old Flexible Flyer. Henry checked my mouth, and said he could see the bright-red nerve dangling inside my broken tooth, like the meat in a cracked lobster claw. I walked home with my sled, and my mother called our dentist, who met us at his office, even though it was Sunday. The dentist studied my tooth, then scraped at it with a pick. The dangling nerve turned out to be just red paint from the sled, so he ground down the chipped part until it felt smooth to my tongue.

Certain body parts may exert a kind of Karmic force. I’ve broken the same arm three times (in falls from a clothesline pole, in nursery school; a playground sand table, in first grade; and a fireplace mantelpiece, in college), and the sledding accident was the second time I’d broken an incisor. The first time, I was two years old and had gone to the grocery store with my mother and tried to hang by my knees from the chrome-plated railing that separated our checkout lane from the next. I lost my grip and fell on my face, and the tooth had to be extracted. In what may be my earliest memory—if it’s an actual memory—a woman dressed in white is holding a black mask and looming toward me, saying, “Breathe deep, Davy.” The gap in my front teeth made me look older than I was until, a few years later, the permanent tooth grew in.

Duncan had a chipped incisor, too. He got his by falling forward after becoming light-headed when a doctor removed a plaster cast from his arm. As always happens, that tooth came to seem like not just an injury but an element of his being—like his hair, or his smile, or the Economy Wagon, or the scar on his right hand. He got that scar at the Burned-Down House one winter. We had spent many hours building a sledding trail through the trees on one side of the hill—piling up snow to form banked turns, and using loppers and pruning saws to remove saplings. On one run, Duncan overshot a turn and launched into a tangle of brush, and the end of a broken branch tore through his glove and up the back of one finger. He ran home screaming, and the rest of us ran behind him, studying the blood trail in the snow.

That same winter, we made an enormous snowball by pushing it back and forth across the sloping field on the far side of the Burned-Down House until it was almost too heavy to move. Then we shoved it toward the road, a broad boulevard that curved around the bottom of the hill, and as the ball moved down the slope it gathered more snow, like a rug rolling up. When it hit the pavement, it slumped into a rounded heap, and blocked one lane. I don’t think we were trying to hit a car. At least, I hope we weren’t: that curve was dangerous even in good weather. On a warm night a year after I graduated from high school, a boy in the class behind mine, whose house was up the street from my house, was killed near the same spot. He’d just left a party, and his car hit a tree, and the scar on the tree’s trunk remained conspicuous for a long time. The boy’s mother and my mother now live in the same assisted-living facility—that’s how long ago this was. And Duncan—who taught himself to play the guitar when we were in high school, and married a woman who was so pretty in grade school that she modelled underwear in department-store catalogues, and got divorced after a few years, and had trouble keeping a job, and had substance issues, and sometimes played music in a restaurant managed by another classmate of ours, and finally met a woman who was just right for him, and adopted an abused dog, and seemed to be getting his life straightened out—is dead as well, of a heart attack, five years ago, when he was fifty-two. And not long after Duncan died his girlfriend died, too.

During the summer of 1971, Henry and I and another classmate spent two weeks backpacking and rock-climbing in the San Juan Mountains, in southwestern Colorado, in what later became the Weminuche Wilderness Area. The trip was organized by an outfit, now defunct, that patterned itself on Outward Bound. There were about a dozen of us, plus two instructors and a dog, and toward the end of the trip the instructors spaced us out along a mountain stream, over a distance of a mile or two, for a “survival solo.” The idea was that, by spending eighty-four hours with no food and no company except our thoughts, we would gain insight into our private depths—while also, possibly, doing the trip’s organizers a favor by sparing them the expense of feeding us for three and a half days. I spread my sleeping bag under a rock overhang and passed most of my time swatting mosquitoes and filling my journal with increasingly pitiful complaints about how bored and, especially, how hungry I was. I wrote the names of foods I wished I were eating, and the list eventually contained what must have been every edible thing I could think of, including fried shrimp, brown-sugar Pop-Tarts, and frogs’ legs.

Sometime during the first afternoon, while I was doing what I described in my journal as “carving a spear for marmot hunting,” my knife slipped and I put a deep gash in the end of my left thumb. If I’d been home, I would have had stitches: when I stuck the thumb in my mouth and sucked, blood filled my cheeks like water from a hose. I made bandages by tearing inch-wide strips from the bottom of my T-shirt, and every time I unwrapped one to see how my thumb was doing the gash reopened and the bleeding began again, as if it had never stopped. I can still see the scar, a slender interruption in my thumbprint, an inch-long arc a quarter inch below the edge of the nail.

Despite all that, when the trip was over I wanted to go back. And the following summer I did, with Duncan. Somehow, we convinced our parents that two not entirely trustworthy seventeen-year-olds ought to be allowed to spend a fortnight in the wild by themselves, without supervision. My father was probably the easiest to convince. Many of his favorite memories involved summers he’d spent, as a teen-ager in the late nineteen-thirties, at a camp in Montana, where he dressed like a cowboy, lived on a horse, and found an Indian flint blade the size of his hand. My mother told me, many years later, that she didn’t know why they’d let us go, with our backpacks and climbing rope and freeze-dried mashed potatoes and no tent; we’d just somehow seemed too determined to be resisted. We almost talked them into letting us drive, too, but in the end they decided that travelling nine hundred miles across Kansas and over the Continental Divide, and back, was probably more than the Economy Wagon could handle. We flew to Denver and then to Durango, and we took a coal-fired narrow-gauge train from there up the canyon of the Animas River, to a whistle stop a few miles south of Silverton, an old mining town. And then, as the sun descended toward the tops of the trees, we started up Elk Creek and into the San Juans.

I came very close to ruining that trip before it began, because my cherry-bomb accident at the Burned-Down House occurred just a few weeks before we were supposed to leave. The wound was on the knuckle, and it pulled open a few hours after the doctor removed the stitches. I said nothing to my parents, because I worried that, if the doctor decided I needed to be sewn up again, Duncan and I would have to stay home. So I tried to walk without using my big toe, and I filled one of the pockets of my backpack with Band-Aids and gauze.

The thought of hiking away from the edge of the known world appealed to me in those days. My principal role model was Al, the leader of Henry’s and my Colorado trip the year before. He had long hair, a beard, a motorcycle, a cast-iron skillet, a reflector oven for baking bread, and a “Keep on Truckin’ ” patch sewn to the flap of his battered Kelty backpack—and I wanted those things, too. I’d often fantasized about running away from home, to become a hippie or a mountain man. Unfortunately, from my point of view, the only serious grievance I had against my parents was that they’d never treated me in a manner that a reasonable person would consider grounds for running away—a logical knot I couldn’t untie.

When I was in tenth grade, I went out twice with a girl who actually did run away, as I discovered one evening when I drove to her house to pick her up to take her to a movie and found nobody home. I was disappointed that she hadn’t asked me to go with her or, at least, told me she was going, but, in truth, I barely knew her, and I never saw her again. The year before that, a friend I’ll call Eddy ran away. He spent one night on the pool table in the basement of the house of our friend Hinkley, and while we were at school the next day he shoplifted a billiard-room talc dispenser from Sears and screwed it to the frame of a door near the cue rack, as a sort of house present. Sometime later, Hinkley’s father noticed talc on the floor and took a sample downtown to be analyzed by the police. This was fairly early in the Woodstock era, when it was possible for an educated adult to believe that ninth graders might be buying heroin by the pound and serving it to themselves from wall-mounted cannisters. Eddy’s father—who, I learned later, sometimes beat Eddy—had darker suspicions. He believed that the rest of us exerted a malevolent influence on his son, and had persuaded him to run away. A day or two after Eddy had given up and gone home, his father stopped his car in front of Henry, who was walking to school by way of the Burned-Down House, and told him that if he pulled anything like that ever again he would kill him.

Wandering around Colorado with Duncan for two weeks seemed almost as good as running away. I’d shown my father, on a topographical map, more or less where we meant to go, but all I really knew was the spot where we planned to get off the train. There were no cell phones or G.P.S. devices or Park Service Web sites in those days, and we hadn’t read a guidebook about the region, and there was no one to check in with before we set out. Today, on Google Earth, I can zoom down on jade-colored mountain lakes that must be ones we camped beside, but at the time I felt almost as though we’d crossed into another dimension, that we had become invisible to the rest of the universe as soon as the train was out of sight. The world seemed much bigger then than it does now. Duncan and I explored canyons, and traversed snowfields, and camped where we wanted to, and climbed a mountain called Storm King Peak.

We talked about my girlfriend, who had recently broken up with me for what turned out not to be the last time, and about his girlfriend, who persisted in refusing to break up with him, and about other people we knew. I ached for the cigarettes we’d decided not to bring. We lay on our backs at night and gazed at the extraordinary stars. I took many fewer photographs than I now wish I had. My toe did fine. We encountered no other people until a couple of days before the end, when we ran into Al and that summer’s version of the group that Henry and I had been part of the summer before. We joined them for the long trek back to the Animas River, along a route we hadn’t been entirely certain about anyway, and rode the train to Durango. Then we flew home to Kansas City and to futures that, like all futures, were too far away to see. ♦
